Motivation

https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2020/jun/04/defund-the-police-us-george-floyd-budgets

Length of stay in host country

  • Paradox: First generation immigrants trust police more than natives, but second generation migrants trusts less than natives (Röder & Mühlau 2012)
  • But “second generation immigrants” is a notoriously noisy (heterogeneous) category
  • Better: Compare within the group of first generation immigrants only
  • Two mechanisms: acclimatization & accumulation of discrimination experiences

Explaining immigrants’ decreasing trust

  • Acclimatization: Reference to origin country important for recent immigrants, but migration experience fades with time
  • Hypothesis: Positive effect of migrating from a country with corrupt institutions should decrease with length of stay
  • Accumulation of discrimination: more time implies more “opportunity” to experience negative contact and discrimination by police
  • Hypothesis: Effect of discrimination increases with length of stay

Police size and immigrant trust

  • Larger police numbers are associated with less trust (Nägel & Vera 2021)
  • For immigrants, more police might also imply higher chances of negative encounters
  • Hypothesis: Immigrants in countries with more police trust less
  • Negative contact should be more likely the longer a migrant stayed in a country with more police
  • Hypothesis: Police numbers effect increases with length of stay

Data

  • Last seven waves of European Social Survey (3-9)
  • Focus on (first generation) immigrants
  • Initial data include 20676 individuals nested in 150 country-years nested in 29 countries

Data cleaning

  • Removed country-years with less than 50 observations (after listwise deletion)
  • Dropped 1021 individuals in 38 country-years and 7 countries from the initial data
  • Covered countries in final analysis: AT BE CH CY DE DK EE ES FI FR GB GR HR IE IT LT LV NL NO PT SE SI
  • Results similar to not dropping any country-years or dropping those with less than 100 observations

Variables (individual-level)

  • Outcome: “please tell me on a score of 0-10 how much you personally trust each of the institutions I read out. 0 means you do not trust an institution at all, and 10 means you have complete trust.” –> The police
  • Variance decomposition: 0.1 is variance between countries, 0.02 is within countries over time, 0.88 is between individuals
  • Treatment: Time since immigration (harmonized in 5 categories) (length of stay (LoS))
  • Moderator: Difference in Freedom House’s Rule of Law score between origin and destination country (institutional improvement)
  • Moderator: “Would you describe yourself as being a member of a group that is discriminated against in this country?”
  • Controls: education, income satisfaction, gender, age, age²

Variables (macro-level)

  • Treatment: number of police officers per 100,000 inhabitants in country per each year (Eurostat)
  • Controls: GDP/capita (Eurostat), Rule of Law in destination country (theoretical range: 0 (worst) to 16 (best); Freedom House), crime rates (homicides per 100k; Eurostat)

Statistical model

  • Hierarchical linear model with three levels (Schmidt-Catran & Fairbrother 2016)
  • Country variables decomposed into within (WE) and between effects (BE) (Fairbrother 2014)
  • WE not plagued by unobserved heterogeneity
  • For cross-level interaction: Genuine within estimator (Giesselmann & Schmidt-Catran 2019), plus random slope (Heisig & Schaeffer 2019)

Summary

  • Individual-level hypotheses confirmed:
  • Longer stay in host country is associated with less trust
  • Stronger effect of institutional improvement for recent immigrants
  • Discrimination effect becomes stronger over time
  • Country-level hypotheses mostly not confirmed:
  • Trust lower in countries with more police (BE), but more police does not lead to lower trust (WE)
  • Police size effect not stronger for long-term immigrants
